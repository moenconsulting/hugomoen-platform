# Map, Compass and Roadmap

Whenever the topic of strategy comes up, I often notice a certain degree of skepticism.

Not necessarily because people are opposed to strategy, but because many teams already feel they know what needs to be done.

They have customer needs they want to address.

Products they want to improve.

Technical debt that needs attention.

Initiatives waiting in line.

The question often becomes:

> Why spend time on strategy, target states, capability maps and gap analyses when we already know what we need to build?

It is a fair question.

I believe the answer becomes easier to understand if we compare it to something most people have experienced before.

A hike in the mountains.

## You Need to Know Where You Are

If your goal is to reach a mountain peak, there is one thing you need to know before you start walking.

Where are you right now?

Knowing the destination is of little value if you do not know your starting point.

Organizations work the same way.

Before we can plan the journey ahead, we need to understand our current situation.

What is working well?

What is not working well?

Which capabilities do we have?

Which capabilities are missing?

What challenges are we facing?

This is essentially what a gap analysis is about.

It helps us understand the distance between where we are today and where we want to be tomorrow.

## You Need to Know Where You Are Going

Once we understand our current position, we need a destination.

On a hike, the destination is the summit.

In an organization, the destination is often what we call a target state.

A target state does not describe every activity that must happen along the way.

It describes where we want to arrive.

For example, how we want our organization, products, technology landscape or customer experience to look in the future.

A good target state provides direction.

It does not mean we know every step of the journey.

But it gives us something to navigate towards.

## You Need a Map and a Compass

If you have spent time in the mountains, you know that it is not enough to know where the summit is.

You also need tools that help you stay on course.

That is where the map and compass come in.

In strategy work, I think of this as a strategic compass.

The compass does not tell us what to do next week.

It tells us which direction is right.

It helps us answer questions such as:

- Why do we exist?
- Who do we create value for?
- What matters most right now?
- How should we prioritize?
- How do we know we are moving in the right direction?

When we face difficult choices, the compass helps us choose a direction.

## Nobody Walks the Entire Journey on a Single Bearing

A common misconception is that strategy means creating a detailed plan several years into the future.

I believe this is one of the reasons many people become skeptical.

Reality does not work that way.

When hiking in the mountains, you do not take a bearing at the start and follow it blindly for six hours.

You walk for a while.

Then you stop.

You look at the map.

You check the compass.

You assess the terrain.

And if necessary, you adjust your course.

Then you continue.

Transformation and product development work exactly the same way.

## The Roadmap Is the Next Leg of the Journey

This is where the roadmap comes in.

The roadmap is not the destination.

The roadmap is not the strategy.

The roadmap is the plan for the next part of the journey.

It describes what we believe are the most important steps right now, based on what we know today.

As we move forward, we learn more.

Then we update the plan.

Some initiatives become more important.

Others become less relevant.

New opportunities emerge.

The roadmap can and should change.

The target state and strategic compass typically change far less often.

## The Problem with a Roadmap Without Strategy

Many organizations use their roadmap as their primary management tool.

That works to a certain extent.

The challenge arises when the roadmap lacks a clear direction.

When that happens, the roadmap risks becoming the sum of every request that comes in.

A little bit here.

A little bit there.

Something for Customer A.

Something for Customer B.

Some technical debt.

Something a leader wants.

Something that seems exciting right now.

Activity may be high.

The pace may be high.

But the direction becomes unclear.

Strategy helps ensure that initiatives point toward the same destination.

A gap analysis helps us understand where we are today.

A roadmap helps us take the next step.

All three are necessary.

## Delivering and Navigating Are Not Opposites

We still need people who build.

We still need teams that deliver value.

We still need action.

But before we start moving, it helps to know:

- Where we are today
- Where we want to go
- Which direction will take us there

Otherwise, we risk moving very quickly.

In the wrong direction.
