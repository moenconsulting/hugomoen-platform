I have worked with platforms in different contexts over the years. Across different organizations, different teams, and eventually across brands and companies as well.

Some things have varied. Many things have changed.

But one thing has remained constant every single time.

Technology is rarely the hardest part.

The real challenges emerge when multiple teams, products or brands need to share the same foundation. When the pace is high, expectations are many, and the platform must both deliver today and adapt to change over time.

At that point, architecture becomes primarily a question of decisions.

Not just what we build, but:

- who makes which decisions
- when those decisions are made
- whether those decisions are still understandable one or two years later

Most platform initiatives start in the right place.

With good intentions around autonomy, speed and reuse.

Teams are given responsibility, authority and trust.

Then what often happens in practice begins to occur.

Decisions are made locally, often for the right reasons and in the right context at the time. But over time, it becomes increasingly unclear which choices were deliberate and which simply emerged along the way.

Architectural intentions become implicit.

Assumptions replace shared understanding.

The result is rarely chaos.

More often, it is something far more subtle:

Uncertainty.

Teams continue doing what they believe is right.

The platform still functions.

But the whole becomes increasingly difficult to explain, and the direction becomes less clear.

At a certain level of scale, tacit knowledge is no longer enough.

Over time, it became clear to me that platform architecture in practice has far less to do with diagrams than with responsibility.

In a shared platform, architecture is not primarily something that is designed.

It is something that is decided.

Which decisions are shared?

Which decisions belong entirely to individual teams?

And where is the boundary between autonomy and shared responsibility?

When these questions are not made explicit, the vacuum is quickly filled.

With local adaptations.

With good intentions.

And eventually with increasing complexity.

One of the most underestimated aspects of platform architecture is traceability.

Sooner or later, the questions arrive:

- Why was this solved in this way?
- Who made this decision?
- Was this a deliberate architectural choice or simply the result of history?

If the answers depend on someone remembering them, the architecture is fragile.

People change roles.

Teams evolve.

Context disappears faster than we think.

Architecture that cannot explain itself does not scale well, no matter how elegant it once was.

I have also learned that autonomy and architectural responsibility are not opposites.

Quite the opposite.

Autonomy works best when the boundaries are clear.

When teams understand:

- what they fully own
- what decisions are shared
- why those boundaries exist

Things move faster, not slower.

Uncertainty is reduced.

Discussions become better.

Architecture becomes a tool for progress rather than a source of friction.

For me, this has become the core of platform architecture, whether working within a single organization or across brands and companies:

Platform architecture only becomes real when decisions are deliberate, visible and traceable.

Not because architects want control.

But because platforms do not scale on assumptions.

For me, this is not about winning architectural debates or creating perfect models.

It is about helping build platforms that continue to work over time, across teams and through change.

I have become increasingly interested in architecture as something practical and verifiable.

Something that survives people leaving, organizations changing and context disappearing.

When decisions can still be understood long after they were made, architecture has done its job.

At least that is the kind of architecture I strive to contribute to, and continue learning more about through practice.
