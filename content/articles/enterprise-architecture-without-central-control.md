Enterprise architecture is often associated with centralization.

Common standards.

Shared governance.

Central roadmaps.

And for a long time, that approach worked.

In my work as an Enterprise Architect at Uno-X, and in parallel as a Domain Architect within a group-wide platform at Reitan, I have found myself dealing with very different, and in some ways opposite, challenges.

In one context, the challenge is creating greater alignment across highly autonomous teams.

In another, the challenge is gradually introducing more autonomy into an environment where governance has historically been stronger.

Yet the underlying problem is remarkably similar in both cases.

Architecture is less about structure and more about decisions, responsibility and clarity.

Which leads to an unavoidable question:

## Is Enterprise Architecture Possible Without Central Control?

I have seen enterprise architecture fail many times.

Rarely because the architecture itself was wrong, but because it was built on assumptions that no longer matched reality.

One of those assumptions is the belief that architectural coherence requires centralized control.

My experience suggests otherwise.

As organizations scale in terms of teams, domains and products, centralized control becomes increasingly difficult to maintain.

The architecture does not disappear.

But it loses effectiveness if it is designed for a reality that no longer exists.

What often follows is familiar.

Some teams are pushed toward more autonomy than they are actually ready for.

Others experience the opposite: governance structures so strong that local ownership becomes difficult.

The enterprise architect ends up somewhere in between, trying to create alignment without becoming a bottleneck.

At that point, architecture discussions often become ideological:

- Centralization versus autonomy
- Control versus speed

In my experience, that is the wrong framing.

The question is not whether enterprise architecture can function without central control.

The question is what kind of control is actually necessary.

Architecture does not require centralized solutions in order to exist.

What it requires is shared clarity.

## Decision Boundaries Instead of Detailed Control

In decentralized organizations, architecture is less about defining solutions and more about defining decision boundaries.

That means being explicit about:

- Which decisions must be shared
- Which decisions belong to individual teams
- Where the boundaries actually exist

When these things are unclear, the vacuum fills quickly.

Not out of resistance.

But out of a need to keep moving forward.

Over time, this can lead to fragmentation that looks like freedom but in reality becomes increasing complexity.

Enterprise architecture fails here, not because it is too strict, but because it is too implicit.

Principles exist.

Intentions exist.

Diagrams exist.

But the consequences of decisions remain unclear.

Teams are told they are autonomous, yet later discover that their decisions have consequences far beyond their own domain.

That is not autonomy.

That is uncertainty.

## From Control to Clarity

What I have learned is that enterprise architecture works best when the focus shifts.

From:

- enforcing standards
- approving solutions

To:

- clarifying responsibilities
- making consequences visible
- making decisions understandable over time

Centralized control does not scale well.

Shared understanding does.

When teams understand which decisions actually affect others, they can move faster, not slower.

## When Control Is Not the Goal

One lesson stands out clearly for me.

Enterprise architecture is not about regaining control.

It is about making decisions clear enough to survive without control.

Perhaps the most important role of the enterprise architect is no longer to design the entire organization alone.

Perhaps it is to help create the conditions under which a coherent whole can emerge.

In organizations with a high degree of autonomy, that means being clear about responsibilities, decisions and consequences rather than centralizing solutions.

When architecture helps reduce surprises and encourage more deliberate choices across the organization, it is doing its job, even without central control.

In some contexts, that also means having the courage to gradually let go of control.

In others, it means reintroducing control where autonomy has drifted beyond accountability.

That balance may be one of the most important architectural challenges organizations face today.
