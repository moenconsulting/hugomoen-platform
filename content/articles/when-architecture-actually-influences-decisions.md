I have never been comfortable with architecture that merely documents what has already been done.

For me, architecture has always been about helping organizations make better decisions.

That has also given me strong architectural viewpoints. Not to be difficult, but because the consequences of architectural decisions are real and often last for a long time.

Yet much of my career has been spent focusing less on architecture itself and more on the work required to ensure that good solutions are understood, prioritized and ultimately chosen.

Many times, different decisions were made.

Not necessarily because they were better, but because they fit the moment better. Because someone was more persuasive. Because the solution was easier to sell. Or because different interests, relationships or political considerations made certain choices easier than others.

## A Recurring Pattern

Over time, I started noticing a pattern.

Solutions that were rejected despite strong architectural reasoning often failed to stand the test of time. They became too expensive to maintain, too complex to evolve, or too fragile once scale and change arrived.

Eventually, often much later, organizations ended up moving toward the very solutions that had originally been proposed.

Over time, that creates trust.

But it is an exhausting way to work.

It is also the kind of struggle that can easily become personal.

When architecture is reduced to preferences and opinions, discussions quickly become a question of who people choose to listen to rather than what is most sustainable over time.

Hidden relationships and unspoken alliances make the landscape even more difficult.

And the harder you fight, the greater the risk that the architect becomes perceived as an obstacle rather than a resource.

## How Does Architecture Gain Influence?

Something changed when I started reflecting on how architecture actually gains influence.

I began to realize that the problem was rarely the architecture itself.

The problem was the form.

Architecture was often perceived as documentation. Something people could read through the lens of their own context. Something they could acknowledge and then continue as before.

That was when the shift happened:

**From architecture as explanation to architecture as decision support.**

## ADR as a Tool for Decision-Making

Working with ADRs became an important turning point.

Not because the format itself is particularly sophisticated, but because it forces explicit choices:

- Which alternatives have actually been considered?
- Why was one option chosen over another?
- Which consequences are we consciously accepting?

Assuming, of course, that the work is done honestly and transparently.

ADRs used as post-rationalization provide little value.

But when used correctly, they make architecture harder to ignore without becoming authoritarian.

## Decision Matrices Improve Thinking

Decision matrices have been an important complement.

Not only to help others, but also to sharpen my own thinking.

When criteria and weighting must be made explicit, it becomes harder for both yourself and others to hold on to a solution simply because it feels right.

## Strategy Must Be More Than Words

The same applies to strategy.

Having a clear architectural strategy, and consistently standing behind it, has been essential.

Even when temporary solutions are necessary.

The difference lies in being explicit about:

- What is temporary
- Why it is temporary
- How we move forward from it

## The Work Around Decisions

Just as important has been changing how I work around decisions.

Good preparation before meetings makes architecture relevant when decisions are actually made.

Fast follow-up after meetings reduces the room for reinterpretation.

And clear communication with the right people often matters more than extensive documentation available to everyone.

## Diagrams as Precision Tools

Diagrams have also taken on a different role.

Not as complete representations of reality, but as precise tools.

For example:

- Simple flow diagrams when clarity and overview are important
- Sequence diagrams close to development teams when interactions need to be understood

Too much detail in the wrong forums creates noise.

Too little detail where it is needed creates misunderstandings.

## From Documentation to Decision Mechanics

Today, I see architecture through a different lens.

Not as a fight to be right, but as the work of making decisions explicit, observable and sufficiently clear to survive over time.

When architecture is used in that way, the need to fight decreases.

Not because disagreements disappear, but because the decision space becomes clearer.

Architecture is no longer documentation of what happened.

It becomes part of the mechanism that determines what actually happens.
